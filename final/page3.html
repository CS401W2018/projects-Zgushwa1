Traditional Mongolian Cuisine

Food in Mongolia reflects the rhythm of everyday life, especially for families who raise livestock and move with the seasons. Meals are made around what's available and what will keep them full and energized. Meat and dairy are mostly found in every dish. In the cold winter months, people often eat hearty meals like boiled mutton to stay warm as it gets -40°C. When summer comes around and the animals produce more milk, their diet will move toward dairy. This season is referred to as “white months”, when foods like yogurt, cheese, butter, and fermented mare's milk, known as airag, are eaten more regularly.

Buuz

Steamed dumplings filled with minced meat. Eaten during Lunar New Year and made by families in large batches.

Khuushuur

Fried dough stuffed with meat. Popular during festivals and as a street food. It's the crispy cousin of Buuz.

Airag

Fermented mare's milk. A traditional summer drink offered to guests in the countryside as a sign of respect.

Other Traditional Dishes You Should Know

Mongolia also has dishes like Tsuivan (stir-fried noodles with meat and veggies), and Boodog (goat or marmot cooked with hot stones inside the animal). These are typically made for holidays.

Drinks include Suutei Tsai (salty milk tea), often served with Aaruul (sour dried cheese snack). These foods are a big part of hospitality and family life in Mongolia.
